Everyday Use Cases at 1kx

Quick wins for anyone at the firm, regardless of role.

These are the most common ways people use Claude in daily work. None of these require technical knowledge — just clear, specific messages.

Summarising long documents

When to use: You have a whitepaper, research report, article, or long email thread and need the key points quickly.

What to paste in: The full text of the document. The longer the better — Claude handles long documents well.

Too vague

"Summarise this." [pastes document]

Much better

"Here is a whitepaper on [topic]: [paste text]. Summarise the key points in 5 bullet points. Focus on: what it does, who it's for, and what makes it different from existing solutions."

Copyable prompts:

"Here is a [whitepaper / report / article] on [topic]: [paste text]. Summarise the key points in [number] bullet points. Focus on: [what matters to you — e.g. technology, team, market, risks]."
"Here is a long email thread: [paste]. Give me a one-paragraph summary of what was discussed and what, if anything, needs action from me."

Explaining complex topics

When to use: You need to quickly understand a new protocol, concept, or market you're not familiar with.

What to paste in: The name of the concept, or paste in a technical explanation you want translated.

"Explain [concept / protocol / term] in plain English. I understand the basics of crypto but not the technical details. Cover: what problem it solves, how it works at a high level, and who the main players are."
"Here's a technical explanation of [topic]: [paste text]. Can you translate this into plain English? Assume I'm smart but not an engineer."

Drafting messages and emails

When to use: You know what you want to say but aren't sure how to phrase it, or you want to move faster.

What to paste in: The key points you want to make, who you're writing to, and the tone you want.

"Draft a [short / formal / casual] message to [who] about [topic]. Key points to include: [list]. Keep it [under X sentences / professional / direct]."
"Here's a draft I wrote: [paste]. Can you improve the clarity and tone without changing the meaning? Keep it roughly the same length."

Structuring scattered notes

When to use: You've taken rough notes in a meeting or during research and need them turned into something readable or shareable.

What to paste in: Your raw notes — they can be messy, incomplete, or in bullet form. Claude is good at making sense of rough input.

"Here are my rough notes: [paste]. Organise these into: key takeaways, open questions, and suggested next steps."
"Turn these notes into a short structured summary with: a one-line overview, 3–5 key points, and any action items. Notes: [paste]."

Comparing options

When to use: You're evaluating two or more projects, tools, or approaches and want a structured view.

"Compare [Option A] and [Option B] across these dimensions: [list — e.g. team, technology, market size, differentiation]. Present as a table. Note where information is incomplete."
"Here are notes on three projects in the [sector] space: [paste]. Which looks most differentiated and why? What are the key trade-offs between them?"

Getting a second opinion on your thinking

When to use: You have a view or a draft and want to stress-test it before sharing.

"Here's my current thinking on [topic]: [paste]. What are the strongest counterarguments? What am I potentially missing?"
"I'm about to send this: [paste]. Does it make sense? Is the logic clear? Is anything missing or potentially confusing?"
