Refining Your Answers

Step 6 of 7

The first answer Claude gives is rarely the final answer. The real power comes from going back and forth — asking it to adjust, simplify, expand, or try again. This is called refining, and it's one of the most valuable skills to build.

When the answer is too long

"That's too long. Can you give me the same answer in 3 sentences?"
"Summarise that in one short paragraph."

When the answer is too short or vague

"Can you expand on that? I'd like more detail on [specific part]."
"Can you give me a concrete example of what you mean?"

When the tone is wrong

"Can you make that sound more casual / formal / friendly / direct?"
"Rewrite that but in a warmer tone — like a friend, not a professional."

When it missed the point

"That's not quite what I meant. What I'm actually asking is [clarify]."
"You've focused on [X], but I actually care more about [Y]. Can you try again?"

When you want a different format

"Can you present that as a numbered list instead?"
"Put that into a simple table with two columns: [column 1] and [column 2]."

When you want Claude to improve your own draft

"Here's my version: [paste text]. What would you change and why?"
"Compare my version and yours. What are the main differences?"

When to start a new conversation

Claude remembers your current conversation, which is helpful for refining answers on one topic. But knowing when to start fresh is just as important.

Start a new conversation when:

  • You're switching to a completely different topic
  • The conversation has gone on a long time and feels tangled
  • You want Claude to approach something without any previous context influencing it
  • You tried many adjustments and it still isn't working — a fresh start often helps

Starting over isn't giving up — it's a strategy. A clean conversation is often more effective than a long messy one.
