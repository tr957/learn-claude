Writing & Comms

Writing & Internal Comms

Memos, messages, slides, outreach — faster and cleaner.

Turning rough notes into a memo

When to use: You have notes from a meeting, research session, or discussion and need them turned into something clean and shareable.

What to paste in: Your raw notes — bullet points, stream of consciousness, anything. The messier, the more valuable this is.

"Here are my rough notes: [paste]. Turn these into a clean internal memo. Structure: one-paragraph summary at the top, key points, and any recommended actions. Keep it concise — under 300 words."
"I need to write an internal memo on [topic]. Here's what I want to convey: [paste notes or bullet points]. Draft a professional memo with a clear structure. Audience: colleagues at a crypto investment firm."
"Here are notes from a meeting about [topic]: [paste]. Write this up as a short memo that someone who wasn't in the meeting could read and understand. Include: context, key decisions or conclusions, and next steps."

Structuring slides and talking points

When to use: You're preparing for a presentation or discussion and want a clear structure before building the slides.

"I'm preparing a presentation on [topic] for [audience — e.g. the team, an LP, a founder]. Here are my key points: [paste]. Structure these into a logical 5-slide outline with: slide title, one-sentence summary, and 3 bullet points per slide."
"Here are my talking points for [meeting / presentation]: [paste]. Organise these into a logical flow and tell me if anything important seems missing or out of order."
"I have [X] minutes to present [topic]. Here are all my points: [paste]. Help me cut this down to the most important things and suggest a clear order."

Drafting outreach messages

When to use: Cold outreach, intro requests, partnership messages, or any external communication where tone matters.

"Draft a short, professional outreach message to [person / team] at [project]. Purpose: [explain why you're reaching out]. Tone: direct but warm. Length: under 5 sentences. We are reaching out from 1kx."
"I want to introduce [Person A] to [Person B]. Write a short double opt-in email introduction — one sentence on each person, why the introduction makes sense, and a clear call to action."
"Draft a follow-up message to [person] who I emailed [X days ago] about [topic] and haven't heard back from. Keep it brief, non-pushy, and professional."

Internal coordination messages

When to use: You need to update colleagues, make a clear ask, or coordinate across the team without writing a long message.

"Draft a Slack message to my team with a brief update on [topic]. Key info: [paste notes]. Keep it under 5 lines and easy to skim."
"I need to ask [colleague / team] to [task or request]. Draft a clear, direct message that explains what's needed, why, and by when. Keep it polite and brief."

Improving something you've already written

When to use: You've written a draft but it feels unclear, too long, or off in tone.

"Here's something I wrote: [paste]. Can you improve the clarity and conciseness? Keep the same meaning and don't change the tone significantly."
"Read this and tell me: is it clear? Is anything missing? Is the structure logical? Here it is: [paste]."
